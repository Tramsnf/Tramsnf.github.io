Artifact 02 | Featured

Career Launch Coach - an AI Career & Resume Assistant

A published ChatGPT custom GPT that helps job seekers turn real experience into strong resumes and confident interview answers.

The Design Thinking cycle I followed to build the assistant. Testing sent me back to revise the instructions before publishing.

Introduction

A no-code AI assistant that gives recent graduates and career changers accessible, judgment-free help with resumes and interview prep.

Description

A custom GPT with a coach persona, a curated resume best-practices knowledge base, and strict honesty constraints. It gathers real experience, rewrites it into results-focused bullets, tailors resumes to a posting, and coaches interview answers with the STAR method. Try the live assistant ->

Objective

To build an AI assistant end to end using Design Thinking, and deliver a genuinely useful tool that solves a real user problem.

Process

Ran the full Design Thinking cycle (Empathize -> Define -> Ideate -> Plan -> Prototype -> Test). Wrote the system instructions, uploaded a best-practices knowledge file, then tested against defined rules - catching a case where an early version invented experience and fixing it with a no-fabrication rule before publishing.

Tools & Technologies

ChatGPT custom GPT builder, Markdown knowledge base, Design Thinking framework.

Value Proposition

Shows the ability to design, constrain, test, and ship a responsible AI product - not just train a model.

Unique Value

Ethics are built into the instructions: it never fabricates experience, never advises on protected characteristics, and reminds users to protect confidential data.

Relevance

Directly transferable to building enterprise AI assistants and deploying responsible, trustworthy AI at work.

References

Live GPT link (above); OpenAI GPT Builder; AI Lab (AIML-500) coursework.

Artifact 03

Machine Learning vs. Deep Learning - A Real-World Comparative Analysis

A written report that compares traditional machine learning and deep learning through two real-world applications, and shows how to choose the right approach for a given problem.

Introduction

An analysis of when to reach for traditional machine learning and when deep learning is the better tool, argued through two contrasting real-world cases.

Description

The report examines customer churn prediction at a telecommunications company (traditional ML) and medical image recognition in hospitals (deep learning). For each case it explains why the chosen approach fits the problem and why the opposite approach would be a poor fit, covering data structure, interpretability, and compute trade-offs.

Objective

To demonstrate the ability to differentiate machine learning from deep learning and to justify model-approach decisions using real-world evidence rather than definitions alone.

Process

Studied the differences in feature engineering, data requirements, and model complexity; selected two contrasting real-world applications; analyzed each for suitability; then argued the reverse case for why the alternative approach would fail. Structured the findings as a professional report.

Tools & Technologies

Technical research and analysis; professional report writing.

Value Proposition

Shows that I can select the right technique for a problem instead of defaulting to the most advanced one - a core judgment skill in applied AI/ML.

Unique Value

Goes past textbook definitions into the trade-offs practitioners actually weigh: interpretability for stakeholders, data availability, compute cost, and whether the features can realistically be hand-engineered at all.

Relevance

Choosing the appropriate approach is one of the first decisions on any AI/ML project, and it drives cost, explainability, and whether the solution succeeds.

References

AIML-500 course lesson; IBM Developer, "A Beginner's Guide to Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning"; AIMultiple, "Deep Learning Use Cases."
